1 · Special Triangles and Their Ratios

Definition — OpenStax §7.2 / SAC Notes

45-45-90 Triangle: an isosceles right triangle. If each leg has length \(a\), the hypotenuse is \(a\sqrt{2}\). Side ratio \(1 : 1 : \sqrt{2}\).

30-60-90 Triangle: if the short leg (opposite \(30^\circ\)) has length \(a\), the long leg is \(a\sqrt{3}\) and the hypotenuse is \(2a\). Side ratio \(1 : \sqrt{3} : 2\).

⚠ Key Reminder: The hypotenuse is always opposite the \(90^\circ\) angle. In a 30-60-90 triangle, the side opposite \(30^\circ\) is always the shortest — use it as your base value \(a\) to scale the others.

Worked Example 1 — Scaling a 30-60-90

Try this yourself first.

A 30-60-90 triangle has hypotenuse 14. Find the short leg and long leg.

Step 1: Identify the side ratio. \(1 : \sqrt{3} : 2\) (short : long : hyp).
Step 2: Hypotenuse is \(2a\). So \(2a = 14 \Rightarrow a = 7\).
Step 3: Short leg \(= a = 7\). Long leg \(= a\sqrt{3} = 7\sqrt{3}\).
Answer: short \(=7\), long \(=7\sqrt{3}\), hyp \(=14\).

Predict: a 45-45-90 has one leg of length 7. What is the hypotenuse?


Answer: hyp \(= 7\sqrt{2}\). Both legs equal \(7\); multiply a leg by \(\sqrt{2}\) to get the hypotenuse.

2 · SOH-CAH-TOA — The Three Basic Ratios

Definition — OpenStax §7.2

For a right triangle with acute angle \(\theta\), label sides as opposite, adjacent, and hypotenuse relative to \(\theta\).

\[\sin\theta=\frac{\text{opp}}{\text{hyp}}\qquad \cos\theta=\frac{\text{adj}}{\text{hyp}}\qquad \tan\theta=\frac{\text{opp}}{\text{adj}}\]

Memory: SOH-CAH-TOA — Sine = Opp/Hyp, Cosine = Adj/Hyp, Tangent = Opp/Adj.

Worked Example 2 — 3-4-5 Triangle

Right triangle with legs 3 (opposite \(\theta\)) and 4 (adjacent), hypotenuse 5. Find \(\sin\theta,\cos\theta,\tan\theta\).

Step 1: Identify sides relative to \(\theta\): opp \(=3\), adj \(=4\), hyp \(=5\).
Step 2: Apply SOH-CAH-TOA.
\(\sin\theta=\dfrac{3}{5},\ \cos\theta=\dfrac{4}{5},\ \tan\theta=\dfrac{3}{4}\)

3 · Introduction to Radian Measure

Definition — OpenStax §7.1

Radian: the measure of a central angle of a circle that intercepts an arc equal in length to the radius of the circle. For a unit circle (\(r=1\)), the radian measure equals the arc length.

Degree-Radian Bridge: \(360^\circ = 2\pi\) radians, so \(180^\circ = \pi\) radians.

Predict: how many radians is one full revolution? Why might \(2\pi\) make sense as the answer?


Answer: \(2\pi\) radians. The circumference of a unit circle is \(2\pi r = 2\pi\). Since 1 radian = 1 radius of arc length, walking the entire circle traces \(2\pi\) radii of arc, hence \(2\pi\) radians.
DirectionMultiplierExample
Degrees → Radians\(\dfrac{\pi}{180^\circ}\)\(60^\circ \cdot \dfrac{\pi}{180^\circ}=\dfrac{\pi}{3}\)
Radians → Degrees\(\dfrac{180^\circ}{\pi}\)\(\dfrac{5\pi}{4}\cdot\dfrac{180^\circ}{\pi}=225^\circ\)

Worked Example 3 — Convert 275° to radians

Step 1: Multiply by \(\dfrac{\pi}{180^\circ}\).
Step 2: \(275^\circ \cdot \dfrac{\pi}{180^\circ}=\dfrac{275\pi}{180}\).
Step 3: Reduce: \(\gcd(275,180)=5\), so \(\dfrac{55\pi}{36}\).
\(275^\circ = \dfrac{55\pi}{36}\) radians

⚠ Common error: A student writes "\(200 \times \frac{180}{\pi}\)" to convert degrees to radians. The multiplier is reversed — to convert degrees → radians you multiply by \(\frac{\pi}{180}\). Correct: \(200^\circ\cdot\frac{\pi}{180}=\frac{10\pi}{9}\).

4 · The Unit Circle

Definition — OpenStax §7.3

Unit Circle: a circle centered at the origin with radius 1. Every point on the unit circle has coordinates \((\cos\theta,\sin\theta)\), where \(\theta\) is measured counterclockwise from the positive \(x\)-axis.

Initial Side: the fixed starting ray of an angle (positive \(x\)-axis in standard position).
Terminal Side: the ray where rotation ends after sweeping through angle \(\theta\).
Reference Angle: the positive acute angle between the terminal side and the nearest \(x\)-axis (always between \(0^\circ\) and \(90^\circ\)).

5 · Evaluating Trig on the Unit Circle

Definition — OpenStax §7.3

For any angle \(\theta\), the terminal point on the unit circle gives

\[x=\cos\theta,\quad y=\sin\theta,\quad \tan\theta=\dfrac{\sin\theta}{\cos\theta}=\dfrac{y}{x}.\]

Special-triangle lengths: short \(=\dfrac{1}{2}\) (30°), middle \(=\dfrac{\sqrt{2}}{2}\) (45°), long \(=\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\) (60°).

Worked Example 4 — Evaluate at \(7\pi/6\)

Find \(\sin\theta,\cos\theta,\tan\theta\) for \(\theta=\dfrac{7\pi}{6}\).

Step 1: Convert: \(\dfrac{7\pi}{6}\cdot\dfrac{180}{\pi}=210^\circ\). Quadrant III.
Step 2: Reference angle \(=210^\circ-180^\circ=30^\circ\).
Step 3: 30° values: short on \(y\) is \(\frac{1}{2}\); long on \(x\) is \(\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2}\). In QIII both are negative.
\(\sin\theta=-\dfrac{1}{2},\ \cos\theta=-\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{2},\ \tan\theta=\dfrac{1}{\sqrt{3}}=\dfrac{\sqrt{3}}{3}\)

Predict the sign of \(\sin(275^\circ)\) before computing. In which quadrant is \(275^\circ\)?


Answer: Quadrant IV. In QIV, sine is negative — so \(\sin(275^\circ)\) is negative. (Reference angle \(=360^\circ-275^\circ=85^\circ\), but exact value isn't a special angle — so it's left as \(\sin(275^\circ)\).)

6 · Reciprocal Trigonometric Functions

Definition — OpenStax §7.4

Three additional functions are defined as reciprocals:

\[\csc\theta=\dfrac{\text{hyp}}{\text{opp}}=\dfrac{1}{\sin\theta}=\dfrac{1}{y}\]

\[\sec\theta=\dfrac{\text{hyp}}{\text{adj}}=\dfrac{1}{\cos\theta}=\dfrac{1}{x}\]

\[\cot\theta=\dfrac{\text{adj}}{\text{opp}}=\dfrac{1}{\tan\theta}=\dfrac{x}{y}\]

⚠ Undefined values: \(\csc\theta\) is undefined when \(\sin\theta=0\) (at \(0^\circ,180^\circ,360^\circ\)). \(\sec\theta\) is undefined when \(\cos\theta=0\) (at \(90^\circ,270^\circ\)). Always check the denominator before computing.

Worked Example 5 — Reciprocals at \(7\pi/6\)

From Example 4: \(\sin = -\frac{1}{2},\ \cos=-\frac{\sqrt{3}}{2},\ \tan=\frac{\sqrt{3}}{3}\). Find \(\csc,\sec,\cot\).

Step 1: \(\csc\theta=\dfrac{1}{\sin\theta}=\dfrac{1}{-1/2}=-2\).
Step 2: \(\sec\theta=\dfrac{1}{\cos\theta}=\dfrac{1}{-\sqrt{3}/2}=-\dfrac{2}{\sqrt{3}}=-\dfrac{2\sqrt{3}}{3}\).
Step 3: \(\cot\theta=\dfrac{1}{\tan\theta}=\dfrac{3}{\sqrt{3}}=\sqrt{3}\).
\(\csc=-2,\ \sec=-\dfrac{2\sqrt{3}}{3},\ \cot=\sqrt{3}\)
